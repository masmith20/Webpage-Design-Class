Tabitha Babbitt

Facts

  • Born: 1784
  • Died: 1858
  • Inventor, Weaver
  • Hometown: Harvard Shaker Community, Massachusetts
    • Shaker: aka United Society of Believers in Christ’s Second Appearing members, who practiced spiritual equality
    • Believed in equality for women
    • Originally called “Shaking Quakers” then later shortened to Shakers

The Circular Saw

              Tabitha Babbitt's hometown benefited from forestry industry, and while Babbitt weaved, she would watch the men working on cutting wood, when she eventually came up with the idea for an easier way to cut wood.

              The problem with the way men were cutting wood was that the Pit Saw only cut when you went forward with it, so the mere purpose of pulling it the other way was to get it back to its starting position. Babbitt saw this as a huge waste of energy.

              Tabitha Babbitt took a circular blade and fixed it onto her spinning wheel. She controlled the movement of the blade by using the pedal on the spinning wheel used to spin the the thread onto the wheel. As the wheel spun and the blade chopped the wood, no extra energy was wasted.

Why Tabitha Babbitt is Overlooked in History

             Although Tabitha broke gender norms by inventing something used in a field that is dominated by men, she still is overlooked in history. Many history books do not dive into Babbitts story, and some do not even credit her with her invention, as people like Samuel Miller also are said to use the circular saw at their mills. However, we must continue sharing Tabitha’s story along with other women like her whose great accomplishments are brushed away in history.

Accomplishments

  • Improved spinning wheel with the double spinning head
  • Invented circular saw
  • Assisted in creation of cut nails
  • Figured out way to cut multiple nails from a sheet of iron instead of putting them together individually
  • Worked on false teeth before her death
